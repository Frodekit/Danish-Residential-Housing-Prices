Denmark's Housing Decade

A decade of data on Danish apartment prices — who benefited, who was priced out, and where young people might still find a foothold near Copenhagen.

“Where can young people still afford to buy housing near Copenhagen, and how has housing inequality across Denmark changed over time?”

Section 01

The National Picture

The animated map shows the median apartment price per m² (DKK) across Danish zip codes. Press play or drag the year slider to explore how prices shifted from 2014 to 2024. The colour scale is fixed across all years — letting you compare directly between any two points in time.

Notice how the Copenhagen area is already deep red in 2014, while much of Jutland starts green. Watch the red spread outward and deepen over the decade.

Section 02

Rising Prices Across All Regions

Every region of Denmark has seen apartment prices rise significantly. The Capital Region consistently leads — but the rate of growth in Zealand and Southern Denmark shows that pricing pressure is spreading outward from Copenhagen.

For young buyers, this is a defining economic reality: the window of affordability is narrowing across the entire country, not just in the capital itself.

Section 03

Growing Housing Inequality

The price inequality ratio compares the 90th percentile apartment price to the 10th percentile across all Danish zip codes. A rising ratio means the gap between the most and least expensive areas is widening.

This trend has direct consequences for social mobility. Young people entering the housing market today face a more unequal landscape than a decade ago — making location-based affordability analysis more critical than ever.

Section 04

Where Can You Still Afford to Buy?

The map shows 2024 apartment prices within a 50 km commuting radius of Copenhagen. The affordability threshold is the national median of 34,524 DKK/m² for Danish apartments in 2024. Areas coloured in green are below this threshold and represent relatively affordable options within commuting distance of the city.

Many zip codes close to central Copenhagen have become unaffordable for most young first-time buyers. However, areas further along commuter rail lines — particularly in southern Zealand, and towards Roskilde and Køge — still show prices below the national median, representing realistic options for those willing to commute 30–50 minutes into the city.

Data: Danish Residential Housing Prices 1992–2024 (Kaggle) • Period: March 2014 – October 2024 • Apartments only
